## I. Macro view

We are in the industrial deployment phase of AI. Hyperscalers have locked in over $650 billion in capital expenditures, and that money has to flow somewhere physical: semiconductor foundries and power generation. The companies that fabricate AI chips and the utilities that keep data centers running are where the spend lands. This portfolio is positioned at those choke points.

The zero-rate era is over. The Fed is comfortable around 3.5-3.75%, and that higher cost of capital rewards real cash flow while punishing leveraged businesses. Meanwhile, international developed markets are outperforming the US by roughly 9 percentage points YTD. Dollar weakness, European fiscal expansion, and BOJ normalization are all driving it. This portfolio captures that.

The client's $400k Google salary functions as a high-yielding bond tied to Alphabet's success. So we don't need fixed income here, and we can't own any more Google. Every ticker below respects both constraints.

## II. Analyst review

I scored all 12 proposals after calibrating against 6 independent grading matrices and correcting for self-grading bias. Top performers: LLM 4 (71/80) for GOOG avoidance and data-backed international thesis, LLM 11 (70/80) for VGT logic and TSMC risk identification, LLM 10 (69/80) for the COWZ free-cash-flow idea. Worst: LLM 5 (43/80) for 30% uranium redundancy, LLM 1 (40/80) for ticker errors.

The 10 rulings:

**1. Bonds:** No. COWZ replaces them. BND at 4% yield is return destruction for a 35-year-old. COWZ screens for the top 100 free-cash-flow generators in the Russell 1000. Low P/E, clean balance sheets, zero GOOG, minimal tech overlap.

**2. International:** Yes. 10% VEA. The US-only crowd is ignoring the biggest macro shift of early 2026. YTD data says international developed markets are winning. VEA captures that without the China/EM tail risk of VWO.

**3. SMH over SOXX:** We are betting on the AI compute bottleneck, not semiconductors broadly. NVDA designs the chips, TSMC fabricates them. They are roughly 40% of SMH. SOXX waters this down with Texas Instruments, Intel, and Microchip Technology, none of which are meaningfully in the AI supercycle. If NVDA or TSMC fails badly enough to matter, SOXX gets hit too. The diversification benefit is smaller than it looks.

**4. Crypto:** 8% IBIT. No ETHA. The halving cycle and institutional ETF adoption are real, but Bitcoin can still drop 60-70%. 8% is honest sizing. 15% is ego.

**5. Nuclear:** NLR over URA or URNM. In retirement accounts, NLR's utility component (Constellation Energy, Vistra) adds dividend ballast and lower volatility than pure miners. The utility contracts are 20-year signed deals. NLR captures both the fuel and the operators burning it.

**6. Small-cap:** AVUV. Factor-engineered for profitability, value, and quality. Outperformed IWM by 300-400bps annually across all measured periods. IWM is out: over 40% unprofitable companies on floating-rate debt at 3.5% rates.

**7. Number of holdings:** 7. Four is hero-trade concentration. Twelve at $83k means $7k average positions, which makes biweekly rebalancing impractical.

**8. Gold:** No. COWZ fills the defensive slot with equity-level returns. At $83k, gold at 8% is $6,640 of insurance earning zero real yield. COWZ at 15% is $12,450 of insurance that compounds. Above $200k portfolio size, gold earns a spot.

**9. Managed futures (DBMF):** No. The concept works, but the math doesn't at $83k. A 20% market drop with a 15% DBMF spike produces $622 in nominal gains. The client's biweekly contributions ($1,150 every two weeks, $27,600/year, 33% of the portfolio) are a far more powerful rebalancing engine. In a crisis, the waterfall method pushes $2,300/month into the most beaten-down position. The salary is the crisis alpha at this scale. At $500k+, DBMF earns an allocation.

**10. VTI:** No. VTI introduces roughly 3% GOOG exposure. Unnecessary when VGT + AVUV + SMH already cover US equities without any Alphabet.

## IX. Bear case

The scenario that kills this portfolio: "AI Winter meets Sticky Inflation."

By Q3 2026, Wall Street decides that $650B+ in hyperscaler capex is not translating to enough software revenue. Enterprise AI stalls at pilot programs. Hyperscalers guide down 2027 capex by 20-30%.

What happens:

- SMH: -35 to -45%. The NVDA/TSMC concentration makes it worse.
- VGT: -25 to -35%. Cloud spending slows. Multiples compress.
- NLR: -20 to -30%. The nuclear thesis gets pushed out 3-5 years.
- IBIT: -40 to -55%. Crypto correlates with tech in sell-offs.
- AVUV: -15 to -25%. Holds up better than the rest but still gets hit.
- COWZ: -10 to -15%. FCF companies with low debt do what they're supposed to do. This is why it is at 15%.
- VEA: -15 to -20%. Holds up modestly if the pain is US-centric.

Estimated portfolio drawdown: -28 to -33%, vs. the benchmark's -16 to -20%. The structural hedge is the client's $27,600/year in contributions (44% of the 401k portfolio). Every two weeks, contributions go into the most beaten-down position. The income is the crisis protection at this scale, not a separate instrument.

Why I accept this:

1. A 33% drawdown on $63k is $21k. Less than one month's gross salary.
2. Biweekly contributions become an advantage in a drawdown. You buy cheaper shares automatically.
3. The AI capex thesis rests on contracted, committed spend with multi-year backlogs. Complete reversal probability is 15-20%, not 50%.
4. The benchmark "wins" by losing less, not by winning more. Over 25 years, accepting 2-3% lower annual returns to cushion one bad year is a bad trade for a 35-year-old.

## X. Conviction score

**8.5 / 10**

One of the syntheses gave a 7.5/10 and flagged that any portfolio with 45% tech, zero bonds, and a 9.5+ conviction score is "marketing, not analysis." I agree. 8.5 sits above 7.5 because this portfolio has a 15% defensive anchor (COWZ), 10% international diversification (VEA), all small-cap growth in tax-free accounts (AVUV), and $27,600/year in contribution flow acting as built-in crisis rebalancing. Below 9.0 because I have to be honest about what 45% tech concentration means in a 12-month window. The per-account target structure introduces minor drift from ideal overall percentages (VEA at 8.3% vs 10% target, IBIT at 9.3% vs 8%), but the operational simplicity is worth it at this portfolio size.

Where the 1.5 points of uncertainty come from:

- AI ROI window (0.6 pts): If software monetization does not materialize by H2 2026, the market can reprice even with continued capex spend. SMH + VGT at 45% is the main tail risk, and SMH's concentration makes it sharper than SOXX would be.
- Political/regulatory events (0.4 pts): SCOTUS tariff rulings, Fed leadership changes, and tech regulation are not modelable.
- No gold or managed futures (0.2 pts): Gold works when equities, bonds, and crypto all fall at the same time. DBMF would add crisis diversification. Both excluded because at $83k, the client's biweekly contributions are more powerful. Above $200k, add 5% IAU and 3% DBMF.
- International rotation fragility (0.3 pts): VEA needs dollar weakness and European expansion to continue. A DXY reversal makes this position a drag.
